Cosmic Inflation

"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose." — J.B.S. Haldane

In the first 10-36 seconds after the Big Bang, the universe underwent an unimaginably violent expansion. Space itself stretched faster than light—a region smaller than an atom inflated to the size of the observable universe.

This isn't speculation. We can see the evidence in the Cosmic Microwave Background—the oldest light in the universe. The tiny quantum fluctuations that existed before inflation were stretched to cosmic scales, and they became the seeds of galaxies.

Regions of space that are now billions of light-years apart were once in contact. They inflated beyond each other's horizons, forever separated by the expansion of space itself.

Exponential Expansion
During inflation, space doubled in size every 10-37 seconds. In just 10-32 seconds, the universe expanded by a factor of at least 1026—a region the size of a proton became larger than the observable universe today.
Inflation Equation
a(t) = a₀ × eHt
The scale factor a(t) grows exponentially during inflation. H is the Hubble parameter, which was enormous during inflation (~1037 s-1). This exponential growth is what made the universe so uniform.
